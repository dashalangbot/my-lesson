③ Vocabulary · 15 minutes

Hobby expressions

These phrases come from authentic texts about hobbies. Learn them — you'll use them in every exercise today.

take up / pick up / give up a hobby
взяться за / подхватить / бросить хобби
PHRASAL VERBS · CORE
Three essential verbs. Take up = start deliberately. Pick up = learn naturally, often quickly. Give up = stop, quit.
She took up knitting during lockdown and never looked back.
He picked up the basics of pottery in one afternoon.
I gave up the piano when I was twelve. I regret it now.
go over the basics
разобрать основы / пройтись по базе
PHRASE · LEARNING
To cover the main principles of something before starting. Works for hobbies, lessons, meetings.
When I take up something new, I always go over the basics with an instructor.
Let's quickly go over the basics before we start the practice.
tackle
браться за что-то / справляться с
VERB · EFFORT
To make an effort to deal with something difficult. Not just "do" — it implies challenge.
If an activity is difficult to tackle, I usually give up too quickly.
We've been trying to tackle this problem for two weeks now.
addicting / addictive
затягивающий / от чего невозможно оторваться
ADJECTIVE · FEELING
Making you want to do something all the time. Addicting is informal/spoken; addictive is standard. Both are used.
Knitting was so addicting that she kept doing it after the lesson.
I didn't expect it to be so addictive — I can't stop!
get it down
освоить / набить руку
INFORMAL · SKILL
To become skilled at something (informal/slang). Suggests you've practised enough to feel confident.
She got it down really fast and was surprisingly good at it.
Once you get it down, you'll love it. Just keep practising.
in the comfort of my home
в уюте своего дома / не выходя из дома
PHRASE · LIFESTYLE
Doing something from home, in a relaxed setting. Often used to contrast with travelling somewhere.
I like doing things in the comfort of my home — no commute needed.
You can learn almost anything in the comfort of your own home now.
have the best time (doing sth)
получать огромное удовольствие от чего-то
EXPRESSION · ENJOYMENT
To really enjoy doing something. More vivid and natural than just "enjoy".
Hobbies I have the best time doing usually involve socializing.
We had the best time doing the pottery class together.

④ Grammar · 18 minutes

Present Perfect Simple vs Continuous

Goal: know exactly when to use each one — and feel the difference.

The forms

PP Simple: have/has + past participle

She has tried five hobbies.  |  I have never given up on something I love.

PP Continuous: have/has + been + verb-ing

Her instructor has been doing it for over a year.  |  I have been feeling great lately.

Five rules — from the lesson

1
Action verb + started in past + still ongoing → PP Continuous

"She has been doing it for over a year." — still doing it now.

2
Stative verb + started in past + still true → PP Simple

"She has had this hobby since fourth grade." — "have" is stative, no continuous.

3
Recent unfinished action with visible result NOW → PP Continuous

"Her hands are dirty because she's been doing pottery."

4
How many / how much? → PP Simple

"She has tried five different hobbies." — counting completed actions.

5
How long? → PP Continuous (unless stative verb)

"How long have you been knitting?"

Side by side

PP Simple PP Continuous
Focus: result / count / experience Focus: duration / process / evidence
She has tried knitting. (experience — did it) She has been trying to get it right. (ongoing effort)
I have given up smoking. (completed change) I have been giving up sugar slowly. (gradual process)

Signal words

PP Simple: ever / neverjust / already / yethow many timesthis year

PP Continuous: for / sincelately / recentlyall day / all weekhow long
