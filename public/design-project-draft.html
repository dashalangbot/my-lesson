01 ·

Problem Statement

When you immigrate to a new country, you face an immediate and urgent problem: life demands the local language before you have it. Job applications, doctor's appointments, lease agreements, school meetings — all of it happens in a language you may barely speak. You didn't choose this gap. You arrived with a full life, a career, an education, a mother tongue — and suddenly none of it transfers.

Standard language learning resources were not designed for this situation. Apps like Duolingo assume you have time, low stakes, and infinite patience for gamified repetition. Community ESL programs assume a generic learner with no prior linguistic framework. Both approaches ignore the most powerful asset adult immigrants actually have: deep fluency in another language. Instead of building on that, they treat it as irrelevant — or worse, as something to suppress.

The result is a structural mismatch. The errors that persist in immigrant learners' speech and writing — article omission, tense confusion, preposition transfer — are not random. They are structurally predictable from the learner's first language. Yet standard instruction almost never explains why they happen, only that they do. Without that explanation, the same mistakes repeat indefinitely, confidence erodes, and learners disengage.

Evidence Base Research consistently shows adult immigrant learners benefit from instruction that explicitly connects L1 and L2 structures (Cummins, 2000; Krashen, 1982). Global adult ESL dropout rates remain high — studies estimate 40–60% of learners disengage within six months of starting a new program (OECD, 2021). Needs assessment conducted through direct instruction with adult immigrant learners (BeLang, 2023–2024) confirmed the three most cited unmet needs: grammar explanation connected to their L1, real-life contextualization, and personalized AI feedback.
~281M international migrants worldwide (UN, 2020) Language gap affects employment, housing, healthcare access globally L1 interference → unaddressed by generic ESL apps High digital literacy in adult immigrant populations

Existing market alternatives — Duolingo, Babbel, YouTube channels, community ESL programs — either lack L1-aware scaffolding, lack specificity for individual interference patterns, or lack interactive AI feedback that responds to each learner's actual production. BeLang addresses this gap directly, for immigrants of any language background, anywhere in the world.

02 ·

Context & Audience

BeLang operates as a fully digital, self-paced learning environment hosted on the BeLang platform. The learning context is primarily asynchronous — learners access lesson modules on their own schedule, from home, often around work and family obligations. This context demands that content be immediately engaging, emotionally approachable, and completable in under 90 minutes per lesson.

The primary learners are adult immigrants learning English, aged roughly 25–55, living in any English-speaking or English-dominant professional environment — the United States, Canada, the United Kingdom, Australia, or any country where English proficiency is a prerequisite for employment or social mobility. They typically have B1–B2 receptive English ability but struggle with productive grammar accuracy, particularly in written and formal spoken contexts. Many have professional or academic backgrounds in their home countries that are not recognized or fully transferable in their new context.

👩‍💼

Primary Learners

Adult immigrants, B1–B2 level, working or seeking employment in English-dominant environments. High motivation, limited time, strong prior education.

👩‍🏫

Instructor / Designer

Solo ESL practitioner with lived immigrant experience, currently enrolled in a graduate-level education program at UPenn GSE.

🏠

Learning Environment

Fully asynchronous, mobile-responsive, no required live sessions. Learners complete modules at home, on phones and laptops, globally.

🌐

Platform

BeLang's own platform, with interactive lesson pages embedding AI-powered practice feedback. Accessible from any device, anywhere in the world.

Equity considerations are central to this context. Adult immigrants are often economically constrained during their first years in a new country, and formal language instruction can be prohibitively expensive. BeLang is designed to be financially accessible, with course pricing significantly below community college or private tutoring costs. Crucially, the platform supports learners' native languages — whatever those languages are — as a pedagogical resource rather than something to suppress. This L1-inclusive approach is both more effective and more respectful of the learner's full linguistic identity.

03 ·

Proposed Digital Innovation

BeLang's core innovation is an AI-powered personal language tutor that adapts to each learner — their learning style, their schedule, their goals, and their native language. Rather than a static course you work through linearly, BeLang learns who you are and teaches you accordingly: when you have time, what you need most, and in a way that actually makes sense given the language you already speak.

Each lesson is organized into tabbed modules covering explanation, visual diagrams, comparative analysis, and practice — all powered by the Gemini API, which responds to each learner's actual written output rather than multiple-choice selection. The AI coach doesn't just check answers; it understands your pattern of errors, tracks what you've struggled with before, and adjusts what it teaches next.

🎯

Adapts to Your Learning Style

The AI tracks how you learn — whether you need more visual explanations, more practice repetitions, or more real-life examples — and adjusts the lesson format accordingly.

📅

Fits Your Calendar

BeLang works around your schedule, not the other way around. Short on time today? A 10-minute focused drill. Have an hour? A full lesson with all five tabs. The platform adapts to when and how much you can give.

🗂

Structured Lesson Modules

Each lesson contains tabs for explanation, visual timeline, comparison, practice, and AI coaching. Learners navigate at their own pace with time estimates per section.

🤖

AI That Actually Teaches

The AI coach doesn't just mark answers right or wrong — it explains why, tracks your recurring mistakes across sessions, and focuses future practice on exactly what you haven't mastered yet.

🧠

L1-Aware Instruction

Every explanation acknowledges why specific errors are predictable from your home language. The AI connects new grammar to what you already know linguistically — rather than starting from zero.

🌍

Real-Life Anchoring

Grammar is introduced through situations adult immigrants actually face: job interviews, medical appointments, texting friends, dealing with landlords, talking to coworkers.

🔊

Pronunciation Integration

Natural audio on grammar examples lets learners hear target structures repeatedly, supporting phonological acquisition alongside written form.

📊

Mixed Exercise Types

Each practice session combines fill-in-the-blank, multiple choice, sentence transformation, error correction, and free production — avoiding fatigue and supporting varied cognitive processing.

Inclusivity is built into the design system. L1 support is available for learners regardless of their native language — the AI coach is instructed to acknowledge the learner's linguistic background when explaining errors, rather than treating every learner as a blank slate. The interface is mobile-responsive, works without login on any modern browser, and requires no software installation. Audio pronunciation is available but never required, accommodating learners in public spaces or with hearing differences.

04 ·

Theoretical Foundations

BeLang's design is grounded in established second language acquisition theory and learning science, as well as frameworks from the course literature on digital innovation and technology integration. Rather than applying a single framework, the platform integrates complementary theories that together address the affective, cognitive, and social dimensions of adult immigrant language learning.

Comprehensible Input
(Krashen, 1982) Grammar is always introduced through real conversational context before formal rule explanation. Learners encounter the target structure in meaningful dialogue before seeing it labeled — meaning precedes form. Lesson hooks use authentic immigrant life scenarios to make the grammar feel necessary rather than academic.
Noticing Hypothesis
(Schmidt, 1990) Target structures are visually highlighted with color, bold, and contrast throughout explanations and examples. The visual timeline and comparison tabs make grammatical distinctions perceptually salient — learners cannot overlook the structural difference between, e.g., "will" and "going to" when it is rendered spatially on a timeline.
Affective Filter
(Krashen, 1982) Anxiety is a primary barrier for adult language learners. BeLang's design maintains a warm, non-judgmental tone throughout. AI feedback is framed as coaching, not grading. Mistakes are explicitly normalized. L1 support — the ability to receive explanations that acknowledge the learner's home language — reduces the vulnerability of engaging with L2 grammar without any familiar linguistic footing.
Contrastive Analysis
(Lado, 1957; Cummins, 2000) Rather than ignoring the learner's first language, BeLang leverages it. Explicit contrastive explanations help learners understand how their L1 maps (and mis-maps) onto English grammar — why certain errors are structurally predictable, not random. This treats L1 as a resource rather than an obstacle, applicable across any language background.
Digital Technology & Learning
(National Academies, 2018) The How People Learn II chapter on Digital Technology identifies personalization, feedback, and motivation as the three critical levers for effective technology-supported learning. BeLang addresses all three: AI-generated individualized feedback, immediate response to learner production, and real-life relevance that sustains motivation across self-paced sessions.
Technology Integration Principles
(Kimmons, 2020) Effective technology integration is not about the tool — it is about whether the tool genuinely serves a learning goal that could not be achieved as well otherwise. BeLang's use of AI is justified precisely here: no static resource can respond to a learner's individual sentence production with contextually specific, L1-aware feedback. The AI creates affordances that are structurally impossible in traditional materials.
Generative AI in Education
(U.S. Dept. of Education, 2024) The DOE's Designing for Education with Artificial Intelligence guide emphasizes that AI in education must be learner-centered, transparent, and equity-focused. BeLang's AI coach is designed accordingly: it explains its reasoning (not just outputs a verdict), it responds in the learner's own language when possible, and it is accessible to learners who cannot afford private tutoring.
Zone of Proximal Development
(Vygotsky, 1978) The AI coaching assistant functions as a digital scaffolding agent, providing just-in-time support calibrated to each learner's production. Rather than supplying correct answers, the AI coach explains what went wrong and why, guiding the learner toward independent grammatical competence — the definition of scaffolded instruction within the ZPD.
05 ·

Stakeholder Engagement Plan

Meaningful stakeholder engagement is embedded throughout BeLang's design and iteration cycle, not treated as a one-time validation step. Penuel (2019) argues that sustainable innovation requires "infrastructuring" — the ongoing work of building relationships, tools, and routines that allow a design to be used and adapted equitably over time. Fischer (2021) frames this through the concept of "cultures of participation," where end users — in BeLang's case, the learners themselves — are positioned not just as consumers of a product but as co-designers of it. BeLang's engagement model is built on both principles.

🔍
Phase 1 — Discovery
Needs Assessment with Learners
Informal interviews and direct instruction sessions with adult immigrant learners (BeLang students, 2023–2024) identified the core pain points: lack of L1-aware explanation, irrelevant or culturally distant examples, no personalized feedback, and anxiety around making mistakes. This data directly shaped all design decisions.
🛠
Phase 2 — Prototype Testing
Iterative Feedback on Lesson Pages
Prototype lessons are shared with active learners via the BeLang platform. Learners complete exercises and interact with the AI assistant, then provide qualitative feedback via informal conversation and written responses. Specific attention is paid to: clarity of L1-aware explanations, relevance of examples to immigrant life, perceived difficulty of exercises, and quality of AI feedback.
🤝
Phase 3 — Community Expansion
Immigrant Community Partnerships
Planned outreach to immigrant community organizations — social media communities, mutual aid networks, immigrant lifestyle and diaspora content creators across language groups. These partners provide both distribution and a channel for gathering feedback from a broader, more linguistically diverse learner sample.
📋
Phase 4 — Ongoing Co-Design
Learner-Driven Content Prioritization
A standing feedback mechanism (short post-lesson survey, direct messaging channel) allows learners to nominate grammar topics they need, report confusion points, and suggest real-life situations for future lesson hooks. This creates a low-friction, ongoing co-design relationship rather than a one-time user study.
Equity Note Stakeholder engagement is designed to reach learners who are often excluded from EdTech user research: working adults with limited time, those without institutional affiliations, and recent arrivals with lower digital confidence. Engagement happens on their terms — through platforms they already use (Instagram, Telegram) rather than formal research recruitment.
06 ·

Scalability & Sustainability

BeLang is designed from the ground up for solo-founder sustainability and iterative scale. Because each lesson is a self-contained interactive page — not a custom-built app with complex infrastructure — the cost of creating, hosting, and maintaining content remains low even as the library grows. The BeLang platform handles enrollment, payment processing, and content delivery, allowing the instructional design work to remain the primary focus.

Scalability Pathways

  • Lesson template can be replicated for new grammar topics without rebuilding infrastructure
  • AI prompts can be updated to reflect new lesson content without code changes
  • Design system allows a second instructor or collaborator to produce new content following established patterns
  • Platform-agnostic output could be hosted on any LMS or delivered through partner organizations
  • L1-support model can be extended to any immigrant language group without redesigning the core platform

Sustainability Factors

  • Subscription-based revenue model with tiered plans — basic access, full AI coaching, and premium personalization at different price points
  • Low marginal cost per additional learner once content is built
  • AI API costs scale with usage but remain predictable and manageable at each tier
  • Core language content remains relevant over multi-year timescales — grammar does not change
  • Instructor's ongoing teaching practice generates continuous insight for content improvement

The longer-term vision includes expanding the lesson library to cover the full range of error patterns most common among adult immigrant learners: article use, preposition selection, conditional structures, phrasal verbs, and register switching between formal and informal English. A companion homework module series is planned alongside each lesson, providing spaced practice that reinforces and extends core lesson content.

Morel et al. (2019) distinguish between scaling up (reaching more people), scaling out (spreading to new contexts), and scaling deep (changing norms and beliefs). BeLang's current phase is scaling up within its initial learner community. The longer-term aspiration is scaling out — adapting the model to new language groups and geographies while preserving the pedagogical core. McDonald et al. (2006) note that exemplary interventions often fail to scale because fidelity to the design gets lost; BeLang's template-based architecture directly addresses this by encoding the design system into replicable, documented lesson structures.

Sustainability is also tied to the instructor's professional development: graduate training in the economics and design of education directly informs the platform's evolution, creating a feedback loop between academic research and practical implementation. The project is positioned to connect with capstone work at the conclusion of the graduate program, potentially expanding into a more formally researched intervention with documented learning outcomes across diverse learner populations.
